## Highlighted research #### Research-Creation: Artificial Nature
I have co-created a series of immersive and mixed-reality art installations over the past decade that present experiences of “nature-as-it-could-be”, through interactive worlds in which participants become just another component within a complex ecosystem. This ["Artificial Nature"](http://www.artificialnature.net) project has been a ten-year collaboration with my partner [Haru Ji](https://www2.ocadu.ca/bio/haru-hyunkyung-ji), currently an Assistant Professor at OCADU, Toronto. Since 2008, Artificial Nature artworks have taken part in over thirty international exhibitions, including festivals such as Microwave (Hong Kong), Digital Art Festival (Taipei), conferences such as ISEA (Singapore), and EvoWorkshops (Tubingen), and venues including La Gaîté Lyrique (Paris), ZKM Mediamuseum (Karlsruhe), CAFA (Beijing) and City Hall (Seoul), and competitive juried events such as Currents Santa Fe, IEEE VIS Arts, and SIGGRAPH (2009, 2013, 2018). - Artificial Nature was invited to submit a chapter in the respected Parisian architecture magazine-book STREAM, published by Philippe Chiambaretta Architecte. [The fourth edition](https://www.pca-stream.com/en/book-magazine/stream-04-the-paradoxes-of-the-living-7) focuses on the paradoxes of the living in the post-anthropocene, and features chapters by renowned philosophers, artists, and architects including Graham Harman, Timothy Morton, Olafur Eliasson, and Joseph Paradiso. Our chapter **["Biotopes Computationnels (Computational Biotopes)"](publications/chapters/17-Stream04-ComputationalBiotopes.pdf)** articulates the motivations that drive the project, including a concern to displace the human from centrally-privileged positions that resonates throughout the book. - The design principles of the project follow a central principle of "endogeny" and "becoming": that all visible and audible elements reflect real behaviours in the simulation with no secondary overlay; that visitors become significant yet inessential parts of the ecosystem; that all parts are in active relationships with multiple others; and that new forms are generated from within. These factors were explored in an article presented at the IEEE Visualization conference, and elaborated in a journal article for **Computer Graphics and Applications** under the title **["Endogenous Biologically Inspired Art of Complex Systems"](publications/journals/16-ComputerGraphics&Applications-EndogenousVisualization.pdf)**. The same underlying principles are given a very different account--through the lens of the philosopher Henri Bergson--in the chapter **["Open Worlds: Bergson And Computational Ontology"](publications/chapters/18-WorldmakingAsTechne-OpenWorldsBergsonComputation.pdf)**, recently published as part of a collection focused on participatory art & architecture. - **Inhabitat** exhibited at a children's science museum over five months in 2017, receiving around 65,000 visitors. An analysis of the exhibit through the perspectives of the artists and the museum educators led to a publication at SIGGRAPH 2018 and also the **[Leonardo](file:///Users/grrrwaaa/code/worldmaking.github.io/yorku_materials/publications/journals/18_Leonardo_Inhabitat.pdf)** journal, currently in press. We are currently developing a new work **Insuperposition** as a direct descendent of Inhabitat, which will exhibit as an invited work at the Daejeon Biennale "Bio + Art", Korea, alongside works by Stelarc, Golan Levin, Philip Beesley, Suzanne Anker and more. - **Archipelago** was one of five works awarded Honourable Mention in the Finished Project category of the [VIDA 16.0 Art & Artificial Life competition](http://vida.fundaciontelefonica.com/en/portfolio/2-special-mentions/). It was also awarded 1st prize in the national Powerstation of Creativity competition, Seoul Arts Foundation (2015). Following its exhibit with the Capitaine Futur show at La Gaîté Lyrique, it became [an emblematic image for the continued manifesto](docs/capitainefutur.pdf) of a Capitaine Futur project -- and we are in discussion for a follow up exhibition in the coming year. Archipelago was also featured in a [six-page full colour spread in Space](docs/SPACE_아키펠라고.pdf), a premier architecture journal based in Seoul. - **Time of Doubles** was featured in, and provided the cover image for, the textbook [뉴미디어 아트와 게임 예술 (New Media Art & Game Art)](http://book.daum.net/detail/book.do?bookid=KOR9788966801534), 유원준 (Wonjun Yu). March 29, 2013. **Endless Current** was selected among 21 projects for the Kaledioscope Virtual Reality Showcase volume II -- an international showcase exploring the art & science of virtual reality, traveling to nine cities and three continents through 2017. An online portfolio of Artificial Nature is linked [here](#tab-works), and a PDF is available [here](https://www.dropbox.com/s/qj9q31fhqov78cv/artificialnature.pdf). #### Software research: live, open-ended, embodied & shared
I am committed to promoting computation as a shared medium of creative experience, which I attempt to support by developing software tools, libraries, and environments that intensify the *liveness* and *open-endedness* of code, and facilitate expressive *embodied* gesture and *collaborative* engagement. This drive is also reflected through the sharing of software tools, libraries, and environments with the wider community, some of which are now in active use by large numbers of artists, engineers, and researchers. - For example, building on doctoral research, I extended the widely-used media art software [Max/MSP/Jitter](https://cycling74.com/products/max/) by co-authoring a new workspace called [“gen~”](https://docs.cycling74.com/max7/maxobject/gen~) that allows rapid development of audio synthesis routines without sacrificing either responsive workflow or code efficiency. Introduced in 2011, it now has tens of thousands of users, including many renowned [artists](http://www.roberthenke.com/concerts/lumiere-2.html) and [musicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autechre), incorporated into courses at several major universities, and utilized within commercial contexts from music technology to automotive industries. The aim to attain open-ended liveness without sacrificing performance has become a cornerstone of my work since. - The collaborative character is also reflected in long-running co-developed projects with fellow researchers such as Wesley Smith and Charlie Roberts. With Charlie Roberts in particular I have contributed to the design and implementation of [Gibber](https://gibber.cc/), now becoming a very widely-used platform for live coding practice, and also co-creating the related [Gibberwocky](http://gibberwocky.cc/) family that connect live coding to more established platforms for electronic music from Max/MSP to Ableton Live to pure MIDI, as documented in publications at ICLC and NIME conferences. Gibberwocky's release for Max saw their highest ever [social media impact](docs/gibberwocky_social_media.png), reaching over 17,000 views within the first three days. With Charlie Roberts I also co-authored a survey of unique challenges and opportunities in live coding for the [The Oxford Handbook of Algorithmic Music](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-oxford-handbook-of-algorithmic-music-9780190226992?cc=ca&lang=en&) under the title [**"Tensions & Techniques in Live Coding Performance"**](publications/chapters/18-OxfordHandbookAlgorithmicMusic-TensionsTechniquesLiveCoding.pdf). - In terms of embodied expression I have focused on simulation-driven augmentation of human gesture. In [**"Incorporating Kinesthetic Creativity and Gestural Play into Immersive Modeling"**, MOCO 2017](publications/conference/17_MOCO_IncorporatingKinaestheticPlay.pdf), a student-supervised project, we presented a novel approach to 3D drawing and sculpting that extends spontaneous human freehand gesture with dynamics-driven simulation, toward more playfully creative interaction. We are developing this idea further with a broader range of physically-inspired simulations, as well as simulations of distributed agency following earlier work in [**"Spatial Interaction in a Multiuser Immersive Instrument"**, Computer Graphics and Applications](publications/journals/13_ComputersGraphicsApplications_SpatialInteraction.pdf). At York University I now direct the [Alice lab](https://worldmaking.github.io), which is dedicated to the development of transferable knowledge, creative coding technology, and computationally literate art practice through the construction of responsive artificial worlds of “deep simulation”, experienced through rapidly emerging mixed/hybrid reality technologies (VR and AR). Research in the lab is supported by around $CAD1m in external funds awarded since joining York. Most recently, an [Early Researcher Award](https://www.ontario.ca/page/early-researcher-awards) (2017) secured five years of graduate funding to pursue a research program that synthesizes the above themes into a project of authoring VR experiences without leaving the immserve context, including augmenting gesture with simulated dynamics, exploring live visual programming in 3D, and blending these with collaborative multi-author networks.

## Artificial Nature: Portrait, 2017 *Pair of Raspberry Pi, 10" screen, Korean traditional paper, 4" speakers.* An audiovisual animation and real-time generative simulation exploring boundary realms between the living and the non-living, familiar and alien, body and world. ## Chronophotography / Chronosculpture, 2013 *Lenticular print, 3D print* Exhibited at: *Media + Life: Sensorial Collaboration.* College of Art, Tokyo Polytechnic University, Tokyo, Japan. 2013-02-04 - 2013-02-07 (Curated). This work reprises Étienne-Jules Marey's technique of chronophotography with its original biological motivation, and yet is entirely artificial. The subject is an artificial life form composed of segmented sections comparable to the class Chilopoda (centipedes), but with trifold symmetric limbs that undulate to propel the organism through a fluid medium. The resulting forms are a product of the organism morphology, the propulsive patterns of its limbs, its predatory macro-behavior, and the viscosity and currents of the fluid it is immersed in.
## Artificial Nature: Time of Doubles / Flux, 2011 *Curved screen, mixed reality, artificial ecosystem.* Time of Doubles is an immersive interactive art installation. It invites visitors to experience mirror existences of themselves taking upon new roles as sources of energy and kinetic disturbance within a perpetually changing virtual ecosystem, a uniquely created computational world. Visitors encounter their doubles in an immersive world through 3D depth cameras, a surround array of active loudspeakers, and projected images. This world displays some characteristics familiar from our own, but is populated by unfamiliar life forms singing, swimming, and breeding through sensitive motions of dark fluids. The visitors’ doubles are energy fields, which emanate myriad bright fluid particles, food sources to be eaten by the virtual organisms. Visitors see, hear, and feel how they are fed to unknown species in this virtual ecosystem. Without visitors, the world-fluid is filled with life seeds that cannot grow, but with human presence the populations explode into alien orchestras of evolutionary growth. Larger organisms hunt smaller ones, leaving physical residues and films behind as they pass, which constrain the fluid flows, and which can be sculpted by visitors’ doubles as they approach them. **Exhibits:** - [GLOBALE: Exo-Evolution. **Center for Art and Media (ZKM) Mediamuseum**, Karlsruhe, Germany.](http://zkm.de/en/event/2015/10/globale-exo-evolution) 2015-10-30 - 2016-02-28 (Curated). - **SIGGRAPH ASIA Art Gallery**. ## Artificial Nature: City Life, 2011 *LED media facade 78 x 99 meters, animation.* - Urban Organism: Triangle Screenscape. Seoul Square media facade. Seoul, Korea. 2011-10-11 - 2011-10-30 (Juried). City Life (도시 생명) is a site-specific generative video for the Seoul Square venue - a media facade of 99x78m located in the heart of Seoul's cityscape. To express a 'world-as-it-could-be' in which city and artificial life coexist, it presents an biological analogue of a metropolis in which the subjects are not citizens, but the city buildings themselves. The significant challenges of this venue include the vast size of the display, the very low resolution of pixels, and the fact that the office windows are often lit. The animation thus includes a population of virtual buildings, allowing a trompe l’oeil effect whereby the actual windows appear to be part of these virtual buildings, emphasized by the high contrast. The buildings represent a human element at a vast scale, but also animate in the form of growing and undulating life forms. Other alien organisms invade the space and consume these buildings, somewhat akin to Kaiju. A toon-shading rendering style is used to minimize the challenges of working at a low pixel resolution. ## Alien Field, 2011 *Generative video* Exhibited at: *PROLOGUE/PROTOTYPE*. ## Genetic Morphologies, 2010 *Generative/evolutionary 3D animation, print* Exhibited at: *Cross-Reality: Traversed & Encompassed Vision.* Arts & Information Center, College of Art, Tokyo Polytechnic University, Japan. Feb 20-22 2012. Genetic Morphology Study is an initial exploration into evolutionary algorithms; a guiding aesthetic prototype for evolving populations of organisms as visual and spatial expression. The intention is to create a biological boundary form between the mathematical and the organic: modular combinations of geometric primitives developed through genetic evolution within a sculptural animation of collective behaviors. Each organismʼs morphology consists of a number of helix and hypercube modules arranged with symmetries varying according to their individual genetic code. The collective behavior of the organism is influenced by its nearest neighbor and the attractions of two ʻstarsʼ. Research-Creation: Artificial Nature

I have co-created a series of immersive and mixed-reality art installations over the past decade that present experiences of “nature-as-it-could-be”, through interactive worlds in which participants become just another component within a complex ecosystem. This "Artificial Nature" project has been a ten-year collaboration with my partner Haru Ji, currently an Assistant Professor at OCADU, Toronto. Since 2008, Artificial Nature artworks have taken part in over thirty international exhibitions, including festivals such as Microwave (Hong Kong), Digital Art Festival (Taipei), conferences such as ISEA (Singapore), and EvoWorkshops (Tubingen), and venues including La Gaîté Lyrique (Paris), ZKM Mediamuseum (Karlsruhe), CAFA (Beijing) and City Hall (Seoul), and competitive juried events such as Currents Santa Fe, IEEE VIS Arts, and SIGGRAPH (2009, 2013, 2018).

  • Artificial Nature was invited to submit a chapter in the respected Parisian architecture magazine-book STREAM, published by Philippe Chiambaretta Architecte. The fourth edition focuses on the paradoxes of the living in the post-anthropocene, and features chapters by renowned philosophers, artists, and architects including Graham Harman, Timothy Morton, Olafur Eliasson, and Joseph Paradiso. Our chapter "Biotopes Computationnels (Computational Biotopes)" articulates the motivations that drive the project, including a concern to displace the human from centrally-privileged positions that resonates throughout the book.

  • The design principles of the project follow a central principle of "endogeny" and "becoming": that all visible and audible elements reflect real behaviours in the simulation with no secondary overlay; that visitors become significant yet inessential parts of the ecosystem; that all parts are in active relationships with multiple others; and that new forms are generated from within. These factors were explored in an article presented at the IEEE Visualization conference, and elaborated in a journal article for Computer Graphics and Applications under the title "Endogenous Biologically Inspired Art of Complex Systems". The same underlying principles are given a very different account--through the lens of the philosopher Henri Bergson--in the chapter "Open Worlds: Bergson And Computational Ontology", recently published as part of a collection focused on participatory art & architecture.

  • Inhabitat exhibited at a children's science museum over five months in 2017, receiving around 65,000 visitors. An analysis of the exhibit through the perspectives of the artists and the museum educators led to a publication at SIGGRAPH 2018 and also the Leonardo journal, currently in press. We are currently developing a new work Insuperposition as a direct descendent of Inhabitat, which will exhibit as an invited work at the Daejeon Biennale "Bio + Art", Korea, alongside works by Stelarc, Golan Levin, Philip Beesley, Suzanne Anker and more.

  • Archipelago was one of five works awarded Honourable Mention in the Finished Project category of the VIDA 16.0 Art & Artificial Life competition. It was also awarded 1st prize in the national Powerstation of Creativity competition, Seoul Arts Foundation (2015). Following its exhibit with the Capitaine Futur show at La Gaîté Lyrique, it became an emblematic image for the continued manifesto of a Capitaine Futur project -- and we are in discussion for a follow up exhibition in the coming year. Archipelago was also featured in a six-page full colour spread in Space, a premier architecture journal based in Seoul.

  • Time of Doubles was featured in, and provided the cover image for, the textbook 뉴미디어 아트와 게임 예술 (New Media Art & Game Art), 유원준 (Wonjun Yu). March 29, 2013. Endless Current was selected among 21 projects for the Kaledioscope Virtual Reality Showcase volume II -- an international showcase exploring the art & science of virtual reality, traveling to nine cities and three continents through 2017. It was also the top-featured project on HTC's Viveport distribution network in March, 2017. More recently, Artificial Nature was also chosen as the central theme of SENSE VI BIOTOPÍA, a widely-documented festival/workshop in Medellin, Colombia.

Software research: live, open-ended, embodied & shared

I am committed to promoting computation as a shared medium of creative experience, which I attempt to support by developing software tools, libraries, and environments that intensify the liveness and open-endedness of code, and facilitate expressive embodied gesture and collaborative engagement. This drive is also reflected through the sharing of software tools, libraries, and environments with the wider community, some of which are now in active use by large numbers of artists, engineers, and researchers.

  • For example, building on doctoral research, I extended the widely-used media art software Max/MSP/Jitter by co-authoring a new workspace called “gen~” that allows rapid development of audio synthesis routines without sacrificing either responsive workflow or code efficiency. Introduced in 2011, it now has tens of thousands of users, including many renowned artists and musicians, incorporated into courses at several major universities, and utilized within commercial contexts from music technology to automotive industries. The aim to attain open-ended liveness without sacrificing performance has become a cornerstone of my work since.

  • The collaborative character is also reflected in long-running co-developed projects with fellow researchers such as Wesley Smith and Charlie Roberts. With Charlie Roberts in particular I have contributed to the design and implementation of Gibber, now becoming a very widely-used platform for live coding practice, and also co-creating the related Gibberwocky family that connect live coding to more established platforms for electronic music from Max/MSP to Ableton Live to pure MIDI, as documented in publications at ICLC and NIME conferences. Gibberwocky's release for Max saw their highest ever social media impact, reaching over 17,000 views within the first three days. With Charlie Roberts I also co-authored a survey of unique challenges and opportunities in live coding for the The Oxford Handbook of Algorithmic Music under the title "Tensions & Techniques in Live Coding Performance".

  • In terms of embodied expression I have focused on simulation-driven augmentation of human gesture. In "Incorporating Kinesthetic Creativity and Gestural Play into Immersive Modeling", MOCO 2017, a student-supervised project, we presented a novel approach to 3D drawing and sculpting that extends spontaneous human freehand gesture with dynamics-driven simulation, toward more playfully creative interaction. We are developing this idea further with a broader range of physically-inspired simulations, as well as simulations of distributed agency following earlier work in "Spatial Interaction in a Multiuser Immersive Instrument", Computer Graphics and Applications.

At York University I now direct the Alice lab, which is dedicated to the development of transferable knowledge, creative coding technology, and computationally literate art practice through the construction of responsive artificial worlds of “deep simulation”, experienced through rapidly emerging mixed/hybrid reality technologies (VR and AR). Research in the lab is supported by around $CAD1m in external funds awarded since joining York. Most recently, an Early Researcher Award (2017) secured five years of graduate funding to pursue a research program that synthesizes the above themes into a project of authoring VR experiences without leaving the immserve context, including augmenting gesture with simulated dynamics, exploring live visual programming in 3D, and blending these with collaborative multi-author networks.

Artificial Nature: Time of Doubles / Flux, 2011

Curved screen, mixed reality, artificial ecosystem.

Time of Doubles is an immersive interactive art installation. It invites visitors to experience mirror existences of themselves taking upon new roles as sources of energy and kinetic disturbance within a perpetually changing virtual ecosystem, a uniquely created computational world. Visitors encounter their doubles in an immersive world through 3D depth cameras, a surround array of active loudspeakers, and projected images. This world displays some characteristics familiar from our own, but is populated by unfamiliar life forms singing, swimming, and breeding through sensitive motions of dark fluids. The visitors’ doubles are energy fields, which emanate myriad bright fluid particles, food sources to be eaten by the virtual organisms. Visitors see, hear, and feel how they are fed to unknown species in this virtual ecosystem. Without visitors, the world-fluid is filled with life seeds that cannot grow, but with human presence the populations explode into alien orchestras of evolutionary growth. Larger organisms hunt smaller ones, leaving physical residues and films behind as they pass, which constrain the fluid flows, and which can be sculpted by visitors’ doubles as they approach them.

Artificial Nature: City Life, 2011

LED media facade 78 x 99 meters, animation.

  • Urban Organism: Triangle Screenscape. Seoul Square media facade. Seoul, Korea. 2011-10-11 - 2011-10-30 (Juried).

City Life (도시 생명) is a site-specific generative video for the Seoul Square venue - a media facade of 99x78m located in the heart of Seoul's cityscape. To express a 'world-as-it-could-be' in which city and artificial life coexist, it presents an biological analogue of a metropolis in which the subjects are not citizens, but the city buildings themselves.

The significant challenges of this venue include the vast size of the display, the very low resolution of pixels, and the fact that the office windows are often lit. The animation thus includes a population of virtual buildings, allowing a trompe l’oeil effect whereby the actual windows appear to be part of these virtual buildings, emphasized by the high contrast. The buildings represent a human element at a vast scale, but also animate in the form of growing and undulating life forms. Other alien organisms invade the space and consume these buildings, somewhat akin to Kaiju. A toon-shading rendering style is used to minimize the challenges of working at a low pixel resolution.
